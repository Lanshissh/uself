The 2030 Deadline

What is the 2030 Deadline?

The United Nations (UN) 2030 deadline refers to the end of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, adopted in 2015. Also called the Paris Agreement, it ultimately aims to take massive action to strengthen the global response to the threat of climate change.

Why Does It Matter?

According to climate experts, we have until the year 2030 to stop the continuous global warming of our planet. If we fail to achieve this, they warn of "irreversible effects" of climate change—more super typhoons, floods, wildfires, and other extreme natural disasters.

Key Statistics
and Facts

The agreement aims to limit global warming to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and pursue efforts to limit it to 1.5°C. To achieve the 1.5°C goal, emissions must peak before 2025 and decline by 43% by 2030. Urgent action from governments, industries, and individuals is crucial to implementing sustainable solutions and reducing greenhouse gas emissions.

• The Paris Agreement has been signed by 195 countries and ratified by 193 as of February 2022.

• Developed countries (especially from bigger countries that have the highest carbon footprint) are expected to take the lead in reducing greenhouse gas emissions by setting economy-wide absolute emission reduction targets.

How are Countries
Supporting One Another?

FINANCE

Developed countries should take the lead in providing financial assistance to countries that are less endowed and more vulnerable.

Technology

The Paris Agreement speaks of the vision of fully realizing technology development and transfer for both improving resilience to climate change and reducing GHG emissions.

Capacity Building

The Paris Agreement places great emphasis on climate-related capacity-building for developing countries and requests all developed countries to enhance support for capacity-building actions in developing countries.

What Will Happen If We Fail to
Meet the Deadline?

Irreversible Climate

The planet will likely exceed 1.5°C of warming in the following decades, making it much harder to control future temperature rise. This could trigger catastrophic tipping points, such as the collapse of ice sheets, permafrost thawing, and many more that cannot be solved anymore.

Biodiversity Loss

As global temperatures rise, many natural habitats may become unsuitable for plants and animals. Species who are unable to adjust/adapt quickly enough may be forced to migrate or face extinction.

More Extreme Weather

Expect more extreme weather conditions by increasing atmospheric moisture and altering weather patterns. Worse heat waves, rainfalls, droughts, wildfires, and many more other threats may become increasingly common. These changes will disrupt ecosystems, damage infrastructure, and put human lives and livelihoods at risk.
